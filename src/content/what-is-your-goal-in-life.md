_I hope it is not happiness._

I, along with many of my friends, am at the stage of life right now where we are asked questions similar to “What is your goal in life?” and “What does success mean to you?” quite frequently (either by ourselves or by others).

A common response is “To be happy.” I think there are two main reasons people say this (emphasis on _I_ and _think_):

1. **It is a cop-out.** Happiness is a common and socially-acceptable goal. Because it is common, people will likely agree with you— at least, no one will hear this and think to themselves, “I do not want to be happy in life.” Because it is socially acceptable, people will likely not judge you or perceive you any differently for saying so (emphasis on _likely_).<sup id="ref-1"><a href="#fn-1">1</a></sup> Even if your true dream is to publish a book, start a business, do an Ironman, etc, it is much safer to say your life goal is to be happy. No one will think differently of you, whereas if you share a grand ambition, they might. Additionally, if you don’t ever publish a book, start a business, do an Ironman, etc., but you never told anyone that this was your goal, no one (including yourself) will think you have failed, which is comforting. People who fall in this category don’t deep-down have the life goal of being happy, it is just easier to tell themselves and others that this is their goal. I would advise them to stop lying to themselves and/or being scared of failure and start chasing their _real_ goals.

2. **They are truly aiming for happiness.** This category of people includes those who are, either passively or actively, searching for/seeking happiness. Happiness is their _goal_, not just a favorable state to be in. There are probably less people truly in this category than one would think, but I also think it is easy to find yourself operating in this category even if you are not truly in it. For the remainder of this piece, I will equate those whose “goal is to be happy” with “those who are aiming for happiness.”

I will begin by illustrating the circumstance in which one might start to aim for happiness and the conflict this creates.

Each time we wake up, we face the decision of what to do with the day, which is correlated with the decision of what to do with our lives. We are frequently told to “do what makes us happy.”

Let’s say the things that make us happy are spending time with friends and eating good food. Does that mean we spend each day hanging out with friends and eating good food? Probably not. Why?

The reason most people immediately realize is that this is typically not sustainable; spending time with friends and eating good food does not pay the bills, quite the opposite in fact.

However, there is often another reason as well, and for this I would like to draw a distinction between two very different things people mean when they say “happiness:”

1. A feeling of momentary pleasure (hedonic well-being)
2. Meaning / fulfillment (eudaimonic well-being)<sup id="ref-2"><a href="#fn-2">2</a></sup>

Spending time with your friends and eating good food would undoubtedly achieve (1) for the first day, and maybe for the next few days, maybe for a week or a month, but would you still feel satisfied each day if that’s all you did (emphasis on _all_) for your entire life?

If the answer is yes, then what gives you hedonic well-being is the same as what gives you eudaimonic well-being. In other words, what gives you short-term pleasure is the same as what makes you fulfilled with your life long-term— congrats! This blog post is not for you. Do what makes you happy, but be wary that this is both a blessing and a curse. Theoretically, you should never have to do anything you don’t find momentary pleasure in to be happy and fulfilled long-term; BUT if you were ever unable to find momentary pleasure (e.g. you could never speak to your friends again or you suddenly lost your sense of taste), you would also immediately stop feeling fulfilled with your life.<sup id="ref-3"><a href="#fn-3">3</a></sup>

Now let’s examine the other side of the spectrum, in which the things that provide you hedonic well-being have no overlap with the things that provide you eudaimonic well-being. Let’s assume that we derive momentary pleasure from spending time with friends and eating good food, but now these things add no fulfillment or meaning to our life, and the things that provide long-term fulfillment require many short-term actions that we derive no pleasure from. In this case, if we were to follow the advice of “do what makes us happy,” we would never make any progress towards what makes us fulfilled long term. And this unfulfillment would, in turn, cause us to be unhappy. But we think the root problem is unhappiness and so we continue to strive for happiness, which obviously solves nothing.

Most of us are neither the first or second case but somewhere between the two. If we spent time with friends and ate good food every day for the rest of our lives maybe we would feel happy but something would feel off. Maybe something would feel missing? Or wrong? It’s hard for me to imagine we could feel happy long term by consistently doing what makes us happy short-term. For one, if we ever were in circumstances that limited our access to these short-term sources of happiness, we would immediately not be happy anymore (scary!). Personally, I would not like my happiness to be dependent on how easy it is for me to spend time with friends and eat good food. Additionally, we will not find long-term fulfillment by always doing what gives us short-term pleasure, and as we become more and more unfulfilled with life, the harder it gets to derive even short-term pleasure from things. One who is fulfilled and aligned with their long-term goals is much more likely to find pleasure in the little things because they are not reliant on them as their sole source of happiness.

Sometimes people (I am one of them) don’t know what makes them happy, apart from the given, trivial things (like spending time with loved ones, seeing beautiful sights, hearing beautiful sounds, etc.). This can cause anguish because “how am I supposed to know what I want to do with my life if I don’t even know what makes me happy.” This logic is quite backwards. Chasing happiness because you are unhappy is like taking cough drops because you are sick. It soothes the symptom, but not the cause.

What I am trying to say is you don’t need to define what makes you happy to be happy. Ask yourself instead, what do you want to _do_ with your life? and happiness will be a byproduct of working towards this. The happiest people I know are those who know who they want to become and what they want to accomplish (an entrepreneur who starts a personal brand, a father who provides a safe home for his family, etc.) and are fulfilled because they are either aiming for or have achieved this outcome, even if the intermediate steps require struggle, discomfort, or sacrifice.<sup id="ref-4"><a href="#fn-4">4</a></sup>

Anyway.

This is not a topic I have thought a lot about, so it is quite possible you will find a counterexample or logical fallacy somewhere here. Nor is it a topic I believe one should think much about on a personal level because it is hard to go anywhere but in circles by thinking rather than doing.

Regardless, all this to say I would like to hear less of “do what makes you happy” and more of “become who you would like to be.”

And also I would like to hear more individual, _real_ goals.
